2. Executive Summary

2.1 Purpose

[0002] It is generally accepted that production lines with widely varying HMI designs will suffer from reduced operator efficiency, which will likely lead to longer downtime events, lower throughput, and reduced job satisfaction. To help counter these issues, the following guidance will establish a set of Human-Machine Interface implementation standards that, when employed by production machinery designers, will establish the consistency necessary to accommodate the specific needs of the production-line operator.

[0003] In addition to the standards established in this document, the guidance will communicate a collection of more generic interface design best practices. We argue that the general level of usability within machine HMIs can always be improved. Accordingly, this document will provide guidance in the areas of usability and graphic design, which should benefit design engineers seeking to expand their background in those disciplines.

[0004] To accompany the recommendations offered throughout, this guideline presents a collection of reference material from which many of the best practices have been established. Readers are encouraged to explore these references, particularly those that fall outside the scope of their primary discipline. Throughout, an underlying theme will become evident; the designer is ultimately responsible for the success or failure of the operator. Put bluntly, the operator's inability to comprehend or effectively use an interface is the fault of the designer. Errors incurred due to flawed design are not the responsibility of the operator, but are instead, evidence of a failure on the part of the designer. It is our expectation that readers of this guidance are willing to accept that responsibility and do their best to optimize their operator's experience.

[0005] Success in the above endeavor, however, is not possible if designers work in isolation. Designers must collaborate with operators to understand their goals and the environment in which they work. Additionally, designers must collaborate with their peers to examine their work with a critical eye and to better understand successful designs and the underlying mechanisms that help achieve the desired outcome. The recommendations provided here advocate these additional steps, as they are essential to the development of optimally usable designs.

2.2 Goals

[0006] As stated previously, this guidance is targeted at designers for the benefit of operators. By improving the environment in which operators work, producers will ideally realize reductions in downtime, increased throughput, lower levels of rework, and operators with higher levels of job satisfaction. Moreover, standardization in specific areas of the human-machine interface should aid in the development of cross-functional production operators that can move from line to line with greater efficiency.

[0007] The best practices offered throughout are provided to encourage continuous improvement on the part of system designers. Our ability to effectively communicate with the operators through the equipment we design can always be improved. The provided guidance is simply a starting point for designers seeking additional exposure to the usability and graphic design disciplines. Proper application of the provided recommendations is ultimately dependent on the context in question; evaluating the resulting designs via operator testing is essential. Getting it right the first time is a lofty goal, but getting it right is paramount. With a greater understanding and ability to control the way operators experience the equipment, it is expected that the designers will likewise experience greater job satisfaction.

[0014] Visual representations of the PackML state model must follow the alignment demonstrated in Figure 2. 3. Background

3.1 Operator Empathy

[0020] In order to appropriately design machines that will ultimately meet the needs of the operator, we must first establish the characteristics that are critical to success.

[0021] The first relevant operational aspect to be taken into consideration is that the operator responsible for multiple machines does not simply stand by and monitor a single HMI. Therefore, when a downtime event is triggered by a specific machine in a complex line, it is highly likely that the operator is not present. Accordingly, effective communication with an operator that arrives after the occurrence of a critical event will prove to be an essential skill.

[0022] The downtime example is further complicated by the urgency of the situation. While poor design is more easily accommodated under normal operating conditions, a downtime event introduces additional stress on the operator. It is reasonable to argue that the likelihood of interface-manipulation errors will increase under this additional urgency. Abnormal operating conditions clearly amplify the impact of flawed design. Accordingly, the designer must take care to consider both the environment and conditions under which the operators perform.

[0023] The associated goals and context in which operators work should be in the forefront of the designer's mind as each HMI decision is made. Understanding and empathizing with the operator is a great motivator for the engaged designer.

3.2 Usability Characteristics

[0024] With a handful of critical operator characteristics understood, it is next important to establish the scope of the term usability. Nielsen (1993) presents a set of usability characteristics to help clarify the concept; the usability of a machine is a function of its (a) Learnability, (b) Memorability, (c) Efficiency, (d) Fault Resistance and Tolerance, and (e) the level of Satisfaction experienced by the operator.

[0025] Within the scope of production environments, system learnability is most relevant for operators interacting with a piece of equipment for the first time. Here, the operator's ability to quickly build fluency in the associated machine interface is a key measure of the designer's success. Similarly, an operator's ability to become productive when transitioning from one machine to another is a function of the latter machine's level of learnability; standardization is a big help in this scenario. It is important to note, however, that expert users (i.e., those executing advanced functionality) will still require specialized training and significant practice before they are able to effectively perform certain higher-level tasks. This is completely acceptable; the learnability characteristic is simply not applicable in these cases. Learnability optimizations are generally not a substitute for proper training and practice. Design techniques like Progressive Disclosure help illustrate this distinction.

[0026] Most easily demonstrated by examining the design of digital printing interfaces, progressive disclosure is a technique whereby expert-level controls and interfaces are segregated from the components that are most frequently utilized. In a typical printer interface, a small number of core facilities (e.g., number of copies, page selection, orientation) are presented in simple dialogs similar to that shown in Figure 5, while more advanced features (e.g., color management, page setup) are hidden in "Advanced" submenus. This separation optimizes the basic interface, providing an environment that is less likely to overwhelm the average user. Accordingly, this technique presents an interface that is highly learnable. The number of options are minimized, leaving a smaller subset of features that must be learned. Expert users instead utilize a separate section of the interface that is dedicated to their more specialized needs. Improving the efficiency for the expert user by mixing more complicated features with basic tools would complicate the interface to the disadvantage of the average user. This should be avoided. Isolating the advanced features is the appropriate design compromise in most situations where mixed audiences exist.

[0027] Learnability is generally measured by presenting new users with an interface-based task. The time required to complete the task is then a reasonable quantitative metric of the system's learnability. When compared to either an existing design or a competing design alternative, the magnitude of the improvement attributed to the new design will help validate the underlying concept.

[0028] In a well-functioning production line, it is likely that an operator will have infrequent interactions with the HMI. When the operator returns to a machine after a prolonged absence, the memorability of the HMI becomes the focal point. Will the operator need to relearn the interface or is there some level of retention that allows a returning user to become productive more quickly than a new user? Testing memorability is generally more difficult than testing learnability as some period of inactivity is required to evaluate how effectively the interface accommodates the returning operator.

[0029] Maintaining the downtime example discussed previously, an operator's ability to quickly identify and resolve an event is a perfect demonstration of the Efficiency characteristic. If instead, the operator must struggle through multiple steps with uncertainty and confusion, the downtime event will likely persist for an unacceptable period. When considered as raw metrics, the efficiency of a production machine is best characterized in terms of the time necessary to complete specific tasks; OEE is rooted in such thinking. While it is often argued that the number of steps taken (e.g., taps, key presses) is a worthwhile metric, we argue that a single step that takes 5 minutes due to operator uncertainty is much less efficient than 50 steps that require minimal thought and take a total of 10 seconds to complete. While lowering the number of required steps is a worthy objective, time to completion is usually the more important goal in production environments. Measuring efficiency often warrants some amount of task repetition. During such testing, the first few iterations will constitute a measure of learnability. Once the task is learned, a more accurate measure of efficiency can be achieved through several additional repetitions.

[0030] Efficiency can often be improved through the addition of shortcuts that simplify frequently used interactions. Standard multi-step operations (e.g., menu navigation or command sequences) can be optimized in ways that benefit advanced users without interfering with novice operators. On-screen touch shortcuts, for example, accelerate the efforts of expert users without cluttering the interface with additional content. While the novice user is still able to employ the standard mechanism, the expert user achieves the same result with fewer steps.

[0031] Likewise, a system that helps operators avoid errors will likely pay dividends in both the efficiency and satisfaction categories. Extra, unnecessary HMI steps performed due to uncertainty on the part of the operator are a prime example of waste when considered from the lean manufacturing viewpoint. Producing designs that minimize such waste accommodate the Fault Resistance objective. Moreover, a system that can efficiently resolve slips or mistakes (e.g., undo to recover from accidentally deleting a product configuration) warrants additional points in the fault tolerance category. Facilities that accommodate fault tolerance will generally improve efficiency and overall operator satisfaction as well.

[0032] While the Learnability, Memorability, Efficiency, and Fault Awareness characteristics are well aligned with quantitative metrics, the Satisfaction characteristic is much more qualitative in nature. The subjective opinions of the operator can be difficult to accommodate. Designing a satisfying interface generally requires (a) a good understanding of the operator, (b) an understanding of the context of use, and (c) an understanding of the operator's goals. When the operator and the associated work environment are at the forefront of the designer's mind, they will be better able to comprehend the subjective envelope of satisfaction. With that understanding in place, proper application of basic design principles will likely yield an interface that is satisfying.

3.3 Situational Awareness

[0033] Given the previously discussed usability characteristics, it is next necessary to consider how a given machine can effectively (a) communicate a condition and (b) direct the operator to the appropriate course of action. Endsley (1995) offers a model for Situation Awareness (SA) that breaks such interactions into three distinct levels. First, the model declares that the operator must perceive a given condition. The associated stimulus could be a horn, a stacklight, a message on a display, a pile of products on the production floor, or the smell of burning plastic. Having achieved Level 1 SA, the operator must then appropriately interpret the situation. What does the light mean? Why are there items on the floor? What's that smell? Finally, after Level 2 SA is achieved, the operator must project the current situation into the future. Perhaps the light is only a warning indicator that requires action to prevent a less desirable outcome. At this stage, the operator has achieved Level 3 SA and must make a decision and, optionally, perform some course of action. Ideally, the achieved level of awareness is not flawed and contains no level of uncertainty. The appropriateness of the operator's response is contingent on the effectiveness at each subsequent awareness level.

[0034] Ultimately, the designer is responsible for ensuring that the situational awareness is not flawed and contains minimal uncertainty. If the operator does not perceive the light because it is obscured from their vantage point, they have no reason to consider its meaning. Or, perhaps, a horn from a different line distracted their attention. Ensuring that the stimulus is effectively perceived is the first challenge. Design principles offered throughout this guidance focus specifically on effective communication.

[0035] Comprehension of the situation with respect to the operator's goals is the next challenge. Given a fully integrated production line and a standard stacklight convention, the operator will be responsible for monitoring the lights on the entire production line. If the operator observes a light transition from green to blue, the operator must interpret that light transition both in the context of the individual machine, and in relationship to the other machines on the line. Once both the observation and comprehension are achieved, the operator will determine, for example, that the wrapping machine is running low on film. Having made this determination, the operator will consider the level 3 SA needs, which address how the current conditions will impact the future status of the line.

[0036] Continuing the example of the machine running low on film, the operator will likely consider how urgent the situation is. If the operator understands that an additional 15 minutes of production can be expected following the first instance of an alarm, the operator will consider this point when choosing a course of action. Perhaps there is sufficient time to complete another task before addressing the active alarm.

[0037] Understanding this basic model of situation awareness is a great benefit to designers. By understanding the basic thought process of the operator, and the relevant levels therein, the designer is left with only a few basic questions when evaluating a design. Will the operator see the stimulus I have designed? If the stimulus is provided on screen, is the intended focal point sufficiently salient, or is there other "digital noise" distracting the operator's attention from the critical indicator? Will the operator comprehend the feedback from the machine, or is the signal ambiguous, leaving the operator with a high level of uncertainty? Finally, does the machine simplify the operator's ability to predict the future outcome? Does the system warrant an explicit message communicating the time remaining until a critical system stoppage? This would certainly help the inexperienced operator that does not yet understand the relationship between system events. When considering the appropriateness of the design, the designer can utilize the situational awareness model to help direct and evaluate potential solutions.

[0038] In the next section of the guidance, we will begin by presenting the PackML Stacklight standard. Throughout the explanation of that standard, we will consider the associated aspects of usability and the components of the situational awareness model that are relevant in each aspect of its design. Once these concepts are considered in the simple context of stacklights, we will continue to explore the more complicated user-interface design domain.

4. Stacklights and Control Push-Buttons

4.1 Scope

[0039] This section of the guidance will specify the mapping between the standard ANSI/ISA TR88 2020 PackML state model – demonstrated in Figure 6 – and the associated stacklight and push-button control implementation standards established here. The mapping will address (a) color coding of stacklight lamps for respective PackML states, (b) related lamp sequences (i.e., flashing or solid), and (c) proper integration of the push-buttons to communicate required operator interactions.

[0040] As noted previously, the implementation guidance in these areas offers a unique opportunity to illustrate the basic usability principles that will be essential in the far more flexible areas of the HMI (e.g., navigation system, user-interface displays). Whereas stacklight designs are naturally constrained to a simple ordered collection of colored lamps illuminated in a specific manner based on the machine state, the graphical display portion of the HMI affords the designer a more limitless range of options, which, unfortunately, offers endless opportunities for the introduction of usability issues. The same simple principles that are inherent in the effective design of stacklights and control push-buttons will demonstrate the foundation of usability in the graphical interface. Discussing these principles in the more tangible context of stacklights and push-buttons may at first seem excessive, but, as we will discover, the design principles that produce optimal usability are quite simple and intuitive once they are understood and embraced. When applied to the graphical portion of the HMI, these principles will improve system usability while requiring minimal effort on the part of the designer.

4.2 Standard Implementation

[0041] The standard PackML stacklight contains four lamps to represent the PackML states described in ANSI/ISA TR88 2020. Ordered from the top down as demonstrated in Figure 7, the lamps will be illuminated in a specific sequence (i.e., off, solid, or flashing) based on the current PackML state. The standard order – derived from the indicator light specification in IEC 60204-1:2016 – presents, from top down, colored lamps in red, amber, blue, and green.

[0042] Under most conditions, only a single lamp in the stack will be illuminated at a given time. However, when a Hold Warning exists (e.g., a material shortage will occur soon, reject bin approaching capacity), the blue lamp will begin flashing while the active state lamp (e.g., red, amber, or green) will maintain its current sequence. Table 2 summarizes the relationships between the stacklight activity, the related machine state, and the associated responsibilities of personnel for respective equipment.

[0043] The associated standard PackML implementation for push-button controls (e.g., start, stop, reset) follows conventions like those defined for stacklights. While adhering to the basic color and marking requirements for push-buttons specified in IEC 60204-1:2016, the PackML standard requires that illuminated push-button actuators use the sequence mapping detailed in Figure 7 and Table 1. The Reset and Start buttons will flash to communicate that the operator must press the respective button to initiate a transition to the subsequent PackML state (i.e., Clearing, Resetting, Starting). Illuminating the Stop button is not required. Also, note that the flashing-green Start button is aligned exclusively with the flashing-green lamp, but that a similar perfect correlation does not exist for the Running condition (i.e., solid-green lamp and solid Start button). Moreover, note that there is no relationship between the blue lamp and the traditionally blue Reset button.

[0044] The underlying state transitions associated with the push-button controls align specifically with the Clear, Reset, Start, and Stop commands defined in the PackML state model. The Start and Stop buttons trigger the associated Start and Stop commands, whereas the Clear and Reset commands are both associated with the Reset button. The implementation of the Reset push-button sequencing (i.e., off, flashing, or solid) will be applied in an appropriate device-specific manner. Following an emergency stop, for example, the system will transition to the Aborted state where an operator-initiated press of the Reset button is likely necessary to trigger the transition to the Clearing state. Similarly, an open door may trigger the Stop command, which will send the machine to the Stopped state where the Reset command is required. If an operator-initiated Reset is required in this scenario, the Reset button must flash. Note that the path from Aborted to Idle (i.e., the path through Clearing and Stopped) requires both a Clear and Reset command, both of which will be mapped to the flashing Reset button. For machines where the full Aborted-to-Idle path can be traversed safely, a single-press reset is permitted. For machines where an operator-initiated reset is not required, the flashing Reset button is likewise not required. In short, whenever an operator-initiated reset is required, the Reset button must flash.

[0045] Figure 8 presents a composite diagram demonstrating the PackML states associated with the red and green lamps. The diagram shows the state flow and the associated transitions of the lamp and push-button light sequences. In the Stopped state, the red lamp will be solid and – if an operator-initiated reset is required – the Reset button will be flashing. When the Reset button is pressed, the system will transition through Resetting to the Idle state, where the Start button and green lamp will begin flashing. Upon a press of the Start button, the green lamp and Start button will display solid through the Starting state to the Execute state.

[0046] When the Stop button is pressed, the green lamp will turn off and the red lamp will display solid. The system will then transition to the Stopped state where the Reset button will again return to a flashing sequence if an operator-initiated Reset action is required.

[0047] Alternately, from the Execute state, for example, the system will transition to the Aborting state when the E-Stop is engaged. On this transition, the red lamp will begin flashing. When the system reaches the Aborted state, the Reset button will begin flashing. Once the E-Stop is cleared and the Reset button is pressed, the system will transition through the Clearing state until the transition to the Stopped state completes, where the red lamp will light solid.

[0048] The sequence demonstrated in diagram Figure 9 will execute on systems that employ the Completing transition. The Complete command will initiate the sequence sending the machine through the Completing state where the green lamp will remain on and the Start button will remain illuminated. When the Completed state is reached, the green lamp will clear, and the red lamp will light. At this point, the Start button will clear and the Reset button will begin flashing. This condition matches the equivalent response that occurs when the system reaches the Stopped state presented as the entry point in Figure 9.

[0049] As demonstrated in Figure 10, the Suspended branch of the PackML state diagram is addressed according to the directional cause of the event. Upstream equipment issues will transition the stacklight to a flashing amber state. Whereas downstream events will trigger a solid amber lamp. With the exception of the Unsuspending state, the Start button will remain illuminated throughout.

[0050] Figure 11 demonstrates the stacklight responses associated with the Held state. The sequence is initiated by the Holding transition (e.g., material exhausted, reject bin full). This state change will disable the green lamp and illuminate the blue lamp solid. The lamp will remain solid through the transition to the Held state. When the Hold condition is resolved (e.g., material replenished, reject bin emptied), the blue lamp will clear and the green lamp will turn solid to signify the transition back to the execute state. Note that the Start button will not be lit within the Holding and Held states.

[0051] The blue lamp will also be utilized to communicate required preventative measures for any internal conditions that would prevent proper machine operation and an associated transition to the Held state. For example, should the machine determine that its local material store is critically low, this condition will be communicated to the operator by flashing the blue lamp. The operator will then have a set period of time to replenish the material store before the machine reaches the Held condition, which indicates that material has been exhausted to the point where production can no longer proceed. As demonstrated in Figure 12, the flashing blue lamp will accompany a second lamp, which indicates the current machine state. The lamps will be lit simultaneously to communicate the Hold warning and the active machine state. If the Held state is reached, the blue lamp will then be lit solid where is will stay until the material is restored to an adequate level.

4.3 Hierarchy, Orientation, Consistency, and Convention

[0052] Given the associated PackML states, the top-down orientation of the lights follows western cultural conventions where the most critical elements are positioned at the top. This cultural convention is evident in the indicator light descriptions of IEC 60204-1:2016, from which this PackML stacklight standard is derived. Here, the Stopped and Aborted states are presented at the top of the stacklight, communicating their importance through their color (e.g., red or amber) and, perhaps more importantly, their position. The sequence attribute (i.e., flashing or solid) further communicates the urgency of the current state. It is accepted that the flashing light will draw more attention than a solid light, therefore, the flashing sequence is associated with the more urgent PackML states (e.g., Aborted is more urgent than Stopped). Note specifically here, that a machine approaching a Held condition (e.g., material low), is considered more critical than a machine that is actively held. Together, the attributes of color, position, and sequence represent hierarchy within the state model. Lights in a higher position are more urgent than those in a lower position, while lights that are flashing are more urgent than those that are solid. Similarly, through cultural convention, red is more urgent than amber. Keep culture in mind when designing as these conventions may not hold in all contexts. Hierarchy in the graphical interface will utilize these same attributes, as well as others, to establish focus, guiding the operator's attention to elements that are most essential to the task at hand.

[0053] Given this summary, a key requirement of the proper application of the PackML stacklight standard is an adherence to the specified light orientation. Across a given production line, it is essential that the order of the lights maintain the top-down orientation shown in Figure 13. While the dual coding (i.e., color and position) within the standard would likely accommodate a mispositioned light (e.g., amber above red), such a violation would surely be a distraction to a line operator. It is expected that a focused color-sighted line operator could overcome a violation of the light position standard. However, under the additional stress of a line stoppage, a simple lack of consistency in stacklight orientation could lead to an extended stoppage. Accordingly, we will highlight consistency in orientation as a key usability principle, one that will be revisited in the discussion on navigation design.

[0054] Moreover, it is essential that the color and sequence attributes of the associated stacklights be implemented in a consistent fashion within a single production facility. While an operator will most likely overcome some level of inconsistency in the size or color of the red stacklights across a number of machines in a single production line, a deviation in the frequency of a flashing light could draw undue attention to a single machine. A machine stacklight flashing at a higher rate will draw more attention than surrounding machines blinking at a lower rate. Such scenarios should be avoided by establishing a standard flashing frequency within the line.

[0055] These points illustrate the relevant distinction between internal and external consistency. Utilizing the same size and shape lenses within a single stacklight of a given machine demonstrates the most basic application of internal consistency. A lack of consistency in a single light of the stack – a different shape or size, for example – would draw additional attention from the operator. This should be avoided unless the additional attention is a desirable effect. External consistency, on the other hand, is demonstrated by using the same top-down sequence of colors in the PackML stacklights across a production line containing machines produced by multiple vendors. When we discuss standardization, external consistency is generally the objective as it accommodates consistency across facilities and organizations.

[0056] An additional application of consistency evident throughout this design is the requirement that at all times at least one of the lamps will be illuminated. This requirement helps eliminate uncertainty on the part of the operator. The absence of an active lamp could lead the operator to believe that the machine is either off or malfunctioning for some reason. Keeping at least one lamp illuminated at all times is an important communication mechanism.

[0057] Finally, while not explicitly discussed in IEC 60204-1:2009, external consistency (i.e., across machines) with respect to the relative orientation of the push-button controls for Start, Stop, and Reset is highly recommended. As with the stacklight example – where color and position combine to communicate state – the dual coding of marking and positioning of push-buttons reduces the likelihood of error on the part of the operator. However, consider the impact of two adjacent machines where the orientation of the Start and Stop buttons are reversed. While inconsistency in orientation should be avoided if possible, the optional introduction of color can aid the operator when positional consistency cannot be guaranteed.

4.4 Alignment

[0058] While it is likely taken for granted in the design of stacklights, alignment is a key component of usability. Operators will conceptually group items that are aligned with one another. The vertical alignment of the stacklights communicates this relationship. Accordingly, it is important to align components that are related; the alignment reinforces the relationship for the operator. Conversely, it is equally important to avoid aligning components that are unrelated, particularly if an assumed association could increase the likelihood of operator confusion or error. Lamps that serve some purpose other than communicating PackML state should be positioned on a separate stack to achieve alignment isolation, which will help avoid distraction and confusion.

4.5 Proximity

[0059] Given the importance of the orientation and alignment principles, it is equally important that the PackML stacklights and push-button controls be positioned in a manner that ensures safe operation and effectively communicates the respective association with a given machine. While operator visibility is a key requirement for effective positioning, a stacklight that is not sufficiently positioned with respect to the machine could fail to effectively communicate the PackML state. Moreover, a PackML stacklight that is not consistently positioned within the relative body of the associated machine may not be appropriately identified if multiple stacklights with similar colors are present within the machine's proximity. Accordingly, it is recommended that the PackML stacklights be positioned such that a line operator with a view of the production line can consistently identify the PackML stacklights for each machine and correctly interpret the machine responsible for a line stoppage or hold event. Stacklights for other machines should be positioned with sufficient separation to ensure their status is not misplaced. Additionally, as safety is paramount, the proximity of push-buttons must be managed to ensure that operators have clear visibility of the associated machine whenever the Start button is pressed.

4.6 Contrast

[0060] The discussions of consistency, alignment, and proximity highlight the overarching importance of contrast. We are instinctively designed to identify contrast. When contrast exists without cause, we can be left confused, searching for the meaning. Contrast can communicate organization through either simple groupings or more detailed hierarchies. We perceive contrast in color, brightness, size, polarity, orientation, and position. Proper use of contrast is essential to effective communication in interface design.

[0061] While it is again expected that sufficient contrast will exist between a lit and unlit stacklight, we will belabor the example to highlight the related design concept. Within a stacklight, it is expected that an individual lamp will provide sufficient brightness to ensure that an operator can effectively distinguish between the lit and unlit states. Likewise, it is expected that the contrast between lamps (e.g., red to amber) is sufficient to ensure that the operator will correctly – without hesitation – identify the active lamps. It is important to note here that the operator's ability to interpret the correct lamp states is a function of both the lights themselves (e.g., brightness, color) and the surrounding environment. Specifically, it is critical to consider the operator's perspective when viewing the lamps. It is possible that the structures behind a stacklight will impair the operator's ability to correctly perceive the active lamp. Proper positioning of all operator interfaces should take into consideration the impact of the surrounding environment and context of use.

4.7 Speaking the Operator's Language

[0062] While it is reasonable to use the conventions of position, color, and sequence to draw an operator's attention to a given machine, it is still necessary to communicate the required course of action in a manner that speaks more directly and effectively to the operator. It is not sufficient, for example, to blink the blue lamp in the stacklight and expect the operator to know what action is necessary. While the push-button controls offer guidance in some scenarios, the graphical interface must effectively communicate the meaning of the blinking blue lamp by presenting an instruction that leads to a prompt resolution.

[0063] Operators that have been drawn to a machine with an effective stacklight indication should be greeted with a message they can appropriately identify and act upon. Hierarchy within the graphical interface is essential for this task. The approaching operator should quickly identify the required action when they scan the graphical interface. Instructions that lack prominence will delay the recovery of the production line. Lack of contrast and emphasis (e.g., size, boldness, color) will reduce the saliency of the operator instruction. Be sure the instruction presented to the operator is easily identified. Testing of the implementation should be employed to ensure the instructions are identified in a consistent manner.

[0064] Avoiding generic instructions (e.g., HELD) in favor of machine specific instructions (e.g., Empty the Under Bin and Press Start) will expedite operator response and line recovery. Where appropriate, utilize conventions like AECMA (Association Européenne des Constructeurs de Matériel Aérospatial) Simplified English, which communicate to a broader audience through grammatical conventions. While the guidance specifically targets English-speaking operators in international contexts, the conventions demonstrate the more broadly applicable importance of establishing language patterns that are sufficient to reduce the required level of language proficiency (Mills & Caldwell, 1997). Similar guidance is offered in the forthcoming Safety of Machinery – Instruction Handbook ISO 20607. As noted by the scope of that standard, if effective communication affects safety, it surely impacts usability as well. In general, interfaces should avoid abbreviations, jargon, acronyms, and engineering-centric language, which will likely be lost on line operators. Reserve engineering conventions for log files, which will not require interpretation from line operators.

5. PackML State Model Representations

5.1 Scope

[0065] This section of the guidance will specify the steps necessary to present the standard ANSI/ISA TR88.00.02-2020 PackML state model in a consistent and effective manner. More specifically, this section focuses on the unique requirements for presenting the state model on a machine's HMI. In keeping with the themes expressed previously, the guidance and associated design requirements prioritize the goals of the production-line operator. The guidance illustrates the typical design challenges that designers must overcome and offers specific recommendations that focus on accommodating the needs of the production operator.

[0066] From the engineer's perspective, the state model – demonstrated again in Figure 14 – represents the underlying control programming that ensures machines across a production line will operate in a correct and predictable fashion. Achieving this level of standardization across the packaging industry has elevated the state model diagram to iconic status; a symbol of the collaboration and harmonization achieved within the industry. Does this representation hold equivalent value for the operator? Likely not. The operator simply needs to understand the current state of the machine and any actions necessary on his or her part. Accordingly, while the traditional visual representation is encouraged where it can be accommodated, a faithful representation is not paramount.

[0067] Fortunately, despite some level of imprecision, the active machine state will be adequately represented by the stacklight and push-button standards established here. The associated HMI components (i.e., lamps and illuminated buttons) visually communicate the current machine state (e.g., Held, Suspended) and direct the operator when command execution is required (e.g., Reset button blinking). With the exception of the solid green lamp, activity is required when any other lamp sequence is active (e.g., Flashing Green, Solid Red). While these components satisfy many of the requirements that might otherwise be communicated through the state diagram, the lamps and the state diagram cannot directly communicate the machine-specific causes of certain production conditions (e.g., bin full, material low); communicating this information via text instructions on the HMI display is more appropriate. To that end, we argue that using precious HMI screen real estate to communicate specific detailed information (e.g., data, trends, operator instructions) is more critical than displaying the PackML state diagram. The associated requirements for a Standard PackML State Model HMI Representation are aligned with these priorities.

[0068] With this scope established, the following sections will outline the specific requirements and guidance related to visual representations of the PackML state diagram.

5.2 Standard Implementation

[0069] Displays presenting the PackML state diagram must maintain the alignment demonstrated in Figure 15. For example, horizontal alignment of the Unholding, Starting, Unsuspending, and Stopping states must be maintained. Likewise, vertical alignment of the Idle, Starting, Execute, Completing, and Completed states must be maintained. If a properly aligned diagram cannot be displayed (e.g., screen size too small) then the full diagram should not be displayed.

[0070] When displaying the state model, all states should be displayed regardless of their implementation in the active PackML mode. If – for example – the Completing and Completed states are not implemented in the current PackML mode, these states should still be displayed in the diagram. Their representation, however, should be muted to communicate that they are not active within the model. Figure 16 demonstrates a representation of the state model where the Completing and Completed states have no functional purpose (i.e., they are pass-through states) and are therefore presented with a reduced level of contrast.

[0071] If the full model cannot be displayed with proper alignment, the simplest acceptable alternative is to merely display the active PackML state. When compared to the example on the left, the redesigned alternative in Figure 17 presents the operator with just as much information; the machine is in the Execute state. That is not to say, however, that the minimalist representation is sufficient. The redesigned alternative in Figure 13 can now accommodate additional information, which will enhance the operator's situational awareness.

[0072] While the specific metrics used to effectively report system state must be decided on a per-machine basis, the example in Figure 18 demonstrates a visualization that has been designed to report both the machine state, as well as other data points that will enhance the operator's situational awareness. These specific metrics are not required and only represent one possible way of effectively reporting machine conditions.

[0073] With these requirements and recommendations in place, the remaining sections will present additional guidance, specific design recommendations, and general visualization theory aimed at effectively presenting the PackML State Model diagram.

5.3 Data-Ink Ratio

[0074] Tufte (1983) argued that the data communicated by a given display should always be optimized and that unnecessary noise should be eliminated. Originally considered in the context of print media, the optimal data-ink ratio is one where every drop of ink contributes value equal to its intensity. If the ink does not communicate any data then its place on the page cannot be justified; zero value, zero ink. Carrying the argument further, if the ink contributes only a small amount of data value it should be presented with a limited amount of visual impact (e.g., small, low contrast). Figure 19 presents a scatter plot redesigned to optimize the data-ink ratio. In this example, the outer border of the plot is completely removed as it holds no data value. The grid lines and axes are redrawn in a less dominant grey color, which replaces the more bold black color used in the original. The individual data points are redrawn without an inner fill, which is more conducive displaying overlapping points while also presenting a more light-weight esthetic. Each of these changes contributes to a higher data-ink ratio. Overall, the impact of non-data-ink is reduced, therefore, the ratio is improved.

[0075] This simple concept facilitated the more heavy-handed redesign demonstrated in Figure 17. Often, it is far too easy to add digital ink to a display. The urgency to fill every empty space with content is natural for those confined within the borders of a QVGA display. However – from the operator's perspective – the majority of the PackML state model is digital noise. The inactive PackML states (i.e., those states supported by the machine, that are not currently active) provide no additional information to the operator. Rendering those states, therefore, is not beneficial to the operator. Figure 17 offers the minimalist equivalent of the state diagram. It satisfies the most important question the complete diagram can: what is the current state?

[0076] The redesigned alternative, however, is not necessarily sufficient as it leaves gaps in the operator's situational awareness. With the space reclaimed by eliminating the non-data-ink, the design can now accommodate additional data that could not be rendered in the previous visualization. Additional questions can now be answered. How long has the machine been in the Execute state? What is the current OEE? How many times has the machine been Suspended or Held since Starting? Presenting such data offers far more value than rendering 17 inactive machine states. As stated previously, the appropriate questions will depend on the target machine and the associated production environment. The Data-Ink Ratio concept simply guides the designer to ensure that the rendered content is focused, first and foremost, on communicating data. Every pixel should be optimized to ensure it communicates either data or nothing. Accomplishing this objective requires a proper application of contrast within the interface.

5.4 Contrast

[0077] An HMI with properly applied contrast will help the operator focus their attention on the areas of a display that are most relevant to the task at hand. An interface that is densely populated with content can still highlight key pieces of data without the noise of supplementary data distracting the operator's attention. Contrast is achieved by varying the intensity of the elements displayed on the screen. For text elements, contrast can be achieved by varying the font size used to display the associated content. Additionally, text can be presented in different colors (e.g., black or grey) to communicate the level of emphasis appropriate for the content. By carefully selecting an appropriate text size and color for each screen element, the interface will communicate hierarchy without the need for heavy-handed visual guides. Considering again the minimalist PackML state diagram presented earlier, Figure 20 presents a simple, yet consistent application of contrast aimed at guiding the viewer's attention toward data and away from "noise". In this example, one could argue that the labels decorating the actual data constitute noise. In order to effectively balance the interface, the design must use text size and color to direct the operator's attention across the display toward the key pieces of data, with minimal emphasis on content that does not contribute to situational awareness.

5.5 Consistency

[0078] Given the previous argument that all non-data-ink should be eliminated, it is reasonable to question the decision to display PackML states that are not supported in a particular mode for a given machine type. Since these states do not communicate any data, the display's data-ink ratio would clearly be improved if they were hidden. In this scenario, however, we argue that establishing consistency of the display should take precedence over the data-ink ratio. The goal of providing the operator a consistent interface while concurrently reducing the data-ink ratio is achieved by reducing the effective contrast of the PackML states that are not represented in the active machine mode. A muted representation for one or more states, as demonstrated in Figure 16, is sufficient to communicate the fact that the associated states are not part of the target machine's PackML State Model.

[0079] Consistency is therefore employed to avoid any distractions to the operator. Maintaining proper alignment within the state model avoids unnecessary confusion. By following the standard, the operator will not interpret misaligned states as something meaningful. The operator will never question why there are gaps in the state model. Muting inactive states is a necessary compromise to both maintain consistency in content and alignment, while concurrently communicating the nature of those states within the model.

5.6 Color Design

[0080] The previous discussion of contrast hinted to the effectiveness of color in highlighting elements on the screen. The example in Figure 20 demonstrates that the pure black text provides the maximum level of contrast against a white background. Demonstrated again here in Figure 21, we see that increasing the level of brightness in a color transitions that color toward pure white, which provides no contrast against a white background. The grey levels achieved in between pure black and pure white allow the designer to provide a level of contrast appropriate to a specific task (e.g., data: black, labels: grey, grid lines: light grey). In the digital RGB (i.e., Red-Green-Blue) color model (i.e., that used by many design tools), pure black is represented as RGB: 0, 0, 0, whereas pure white is represented as RGB: 255, 255, 255.

[0081] When a specific hue is selected, like the cyan background of our sample PackML diagram, the level of impact contributed by the color is correlated to the saturation level. Figure 22 demonstrates the impact of reducing the saturation of a cyan hue (i.e., RGB: 0, 217, 255).

[0082] When used together, the hue, saturation, and brightness (HSB) properties provide a convenient alternative to the more common RGB color model as the HSB model provides a more intuitive approach to color design. Given a specific color, adjustments to the saturation and brightness properties will produce a second color that appropriately complements the original, while being more subdued or more bold. Figure 23 demonstrates some of the shades and tints that can be produced from the pure red sample color.

[0083] Most dedicated design tools offer access to the RGB and HSB models. This facilitates design of a color palette using the HSB model, with concurrent access to the associated RGB values required by most development platforms. Figure 24 demonstrates the color design tools offered by Adobe Illustrator. Similar tools exist in Paint.Net and Inkscape.

[0084] Careful tuning of the saturation and brightness properties produced the inactive-state colors employed in Figure 25. These shades provide high levels of contrast when applied together while maintaining harmony with the original hue.

[0085] In general, highly saturated colors should be reserved for the select interface components requiring the most attention. In this example (i.e., the background colors of the PackML state diagram) it is difficult to argue that such a highly saturated color is warranted. While there is sufficient contrast between the active state (i.e., Execute) and the background, a more neutral background is likely warranted. In this situation a more desaturated color would make a more suitable backdrop for the state diagram. 5.7 Typography

[0090] When presenting text on screen, the designer can employ a few simple techniques to help maximize readability and establish the hierarchy that will guide the viewer effectively and efficiently through the screen's content. The example in Figure 20 demonstrates how varying the relative size of text elements can help construct this hierarchy. As expected, larger text draws more attention. The primary focal point in the example – the text presenting the PackML Execute state – is highlighted using a relatively dominant 52-point text size. While this same text size is not utilized for other purposes in this specific screen, it should be reused consistently elsewhere whenever a dominant focal point is required.

[0091] The importance of this primary data display is further highlighted by a distinct pair of smaller text elements – each dedicated to a specific purpose – that bring order and communicate purpose for the remaining content. This unique combination of text sizes (i.e., 16 pt and 29 pt in grey and dark grey respectively) introduces a pattern that the viewer can anticipate and easily interpret. By consistently using these same sizes for the same purpose in the other areas of the HMI, the viewer will quickly identify data values and labels in a more instinctive manner. This improves both the learnability and memorability qualities of the interface.

[0092] Instead of using an arbitrary collection of text sizes, the example employs typographic scaling, which brings harmony to the display using a fixed multiplier that builds each successive text size from the last. The scale ensures that there is sufficient visual contrast between the display's effective levels (e.g., machine state, data values, data labels) such that the underlying hierarchy is more easily interpreted. Figure 31 presents some alternative typographic scales to help illustrate the concept.

[0093] An effective typographic scale is established by first identifying the smallest effective text size. In this sample, a modest 16 pt text size has been selected. In keeping with the data-ink ratio concept, this minimum text size will be used to present the least data-rich content (i.e., data labels). In practice, the appropriate minimum text size will be impacted by the size of the screen, the viewing distance and angle, the quality of the display, and several other context-specific variables that impact readability. Once the appropriate minimum size is determined, a fixed multiplier is selected to provide a linear increase in the text size of subsequent levels. A relatively large multiplier of 1.8x has been chosen in this example. With only 4 pieces of information, the interface can accommodate a larger scale than would otherwise be available on a display with more densely populated content.

[0094] The smallest scale in the example (i.e., 1.3x) provides less natural hierarchy via size. The hierarchy is enhanced through color, where the smallest text is presented in grey to produce additional contrast. At a scale of 1.8x, the hierarchy is more pronounced. The grey coloring on the data label is still utilized as there is no reason to increase the emphasis on that content. Progressing to the 2.5x scale shows more dynamic contrast, which is certainly more pronounced, but does not necessarily produce a more functional or aesthetically rich display. Altering the typeface itself, however, does offer an opportunity to improve both the aesthetic and functional characteristics of the interface.

[0095] Figure 32 illustrates some of the flexibility offered by various typefaces. While Arial Regular and Halis Rounded Book offer similar personalities, variants like Arial Narrow help illustrate the functional benefits that some alternative fonts can afford. With a more compact body, Arial Narrow requires less horizontal space to display equivalent content without sacrificing height and, therefore, legibility. The demonstrated Halis fonts help illustrate the dynamic contrast afforded by light weight and small cap fonts. Light fonts are simply the opposite of more commonly utilized bold fonts. An alternative to smaller font sizes or lower contrast colors, a light font positioned adjacent to a regular font will likely draw less attention, while adding a clean and efficient aesthetic to the display. The small caps variants offer another method for drawing extra attention to an element. While it is more difficult to read content that is capitalized, it will draw extra attention from the viewer. Accordingly, caps and small caps should be avoided for longer text segments, but are appropriate for alarms and warnings.

[0096] Figure 33 illustrates the differences between a true small caps font displaying the letters "Execute" and a normal font displaying the characters "EXECUTE". The illustration shows the the small caps font produces a larger leading letter "E", while displaying smaller capital letters for the remainder of the word. Contrasted with the fully capitalized "EXECUTE" rendered in the normal font, we see that the more horizontal space is used and that there is more space between the individual letters. The small caps font is specifically designed to provide a more aesthetically pleasing rendering of the fully capitalized equipment. With less space between the letters, the small-caps version produces a visual that appears to be more fluid.

[0097] When longer runs of text are required, it is important to consider line spacing and line length. It is generally accepted that longer line lengths slow down reading speed. Additionally, it is more difficult to read text where the distance between lines is too small. Figure 34 demonstrates the impact line height has on legibility. Utilizing the preferred 120% guideline produces a more readable body of text.

[0098] While efficient use of screen space is a lofty goal, it should not come at the expense of the operator. Poor legibility will impair operator performance, particularly under stressful conditions. Accordingly, line height should be optimized whenever the underlying design tools will accommodate such control.

6. Navigation Design

6.1 Scope

[0099] For most production operators, time spent manipulating an HMI is time wasted. Engineers that design machine interfaces have an important responsibility to ensure that their operators perform effectively. If an operator is not effective when utilizing a designer's interface, it is the designer who has failed, not the operator. In an effort to increase the likelihood of success for our fellow designers, this section offers a standard graphical interface template tailored specifically for use in production environments. Isolated machine-level testing has demonstrated that the following guidelines offer measurable usability benefits, with further improvements expected when the practices are applied across full production lines.

[0100] Understandably, given the vast array of facilities in a typical interface, establishing an appropriate scope for this section of the standard was the first critical milestone. When compared to the relatively finite number of possible variations available in the stacklight standard – where machine state is succinctly represented with nothing more than color, position, and frequency – the main graphical interface on a production machine offers a wider landscape for competing design alternatives. Unfortunately, this flexibility often leads to haphazard designs where little to no consistency exists between machines. In many ways, the less complicated, yet more rigid, stacklight standard illustrates the ideal scope for use here in the graphical interface section.

[0101] Considering the situational awareness model once again, we are reminded that an effective interface a) communicates the state of the world, b) affords insight into future conditions, and c) aids the operator's decision making process. The PackML stacklight offers situational awareness with incredible efficiency. A single flashing blue lamp, for example, is all that is needed to inform a remote line operator that an internal machine issue must be addressed. Ironically, it is the stacklight's inherent constraints that facilitate this situational awareness in such a usable manner. Despite the obvious limitations, a PackML stacklight is effectively a 4-pixel display, where:

  • Each pixel has a fixed color; the blue pixel can only every be blue, and no other pixel may ever use that same color.
  • The pixels are aligned vertically so that their orientation is the same regardless of the operator's viewing angle.
  • Each pixel has a precise meaning that, once defined, does not change.

These limitations and the resulting consistency are paramount to the stacklight's effectiveness.

[0102] Carrying the analogy to the opposite extreme, the HMI terminal is nothing more than a 2-dimensional stacklight; it is reasonable to argue that the same benefits should be easily achieved. Unfortunately, the potential for chaos is far greater in the graphical domain. Whereas the stacklight's physical design provides natural hierarchy and content alignment, the graphical interface demands no such structure. Pixels are easily combined to form shapes and words that are arbitrarily sized and positioned on the display. These pixels quickly become rare commodities; there is always more content, never enough space. Content is then squeezed in here, crammed in there. Accordingly, a survey of machine interfaces in a typical production line will likely show little or no consistency in either content or alignment. Worse still, in a graphical interface, the content and alignment may very well change dynamically as the operator interacts with the screen. The HMI working group began their effort with these challenges in mind.

[0103] From the earliest stages of the standardization effort, agreement existed that the graphical-interface portion of the PackML HMI standard would need to be narrowly focused. It was argued that the scope should be limited to the small intersection of operator-interface components that are most commonly encountered on production equipment; expanding standardization beyond this boundary would artificially limit designers and inhibit innovation. Moreover, since no single design will ever offer the perfect interface for all operators, the HMI working group decided to avoid a ground-up design effort and chose, instead, to consider the merits of a selected set of existing design alternatives; each either currently in use or proposed for use in a production line.

[0104] In an effort to quantify the effectiveness of these sample interfaces, the group conducted a formal usability study, which aimed to produce an unbiased evaluation of the respective systems. The study presented participants with a collection of interfaces, each of which organized screen content in a unique and intriguing manner. Participants were challenged to demonstrate basic situational awareness by reporting system information available within each interface. Following these exercises, participants offered qualitative feedback on the respective designs, noting general preferences and providing comments on perceived usability. The study results and subsequent review by the committee left the originally expressed opinions on scope largely unchanged. While the standard HMI template would have a relatively limited scope, a supplementary collection of design guidelines would be provided to help designers produce more effective interfaces without inhibiting innovation.

[0105] Returning to the high-level HMI standardization goals – where the efficiency and effectiveness of line operators is the overarching priority – the effective transition from one interface to the next represents the primary benefit of the HMI template. This objective suggests that learnability, memorability, and efficiency are the key usability characteristics driving the template's design. Accordingly, it is reasonable to argue that the ideal template: a) follows expected conventions, b) maximizes consistency between machines, and c) ensures the operator feels they are in control. Given these criteria, the standard PackML HMI template:

  • establishes a set of standard navigation elements
  • specifies standard alignment and orientation patterns for these elements
  • defines the auxiliary content operators rely on to make decisions in a production

[0106] The standard navigation elements represent the most frequently encountered menus and content groups found in production equipment. Following the best practices illustrated by the analysis of the stacklight design, each element is assigned a fixed position in the HMI's menu along with a standard name and icon representing the associated context. The template's alignment and orientation patterns are derived from the key findings of the usability study, which offered evidence supporting established interface conventions from existing production HMIs. Concurrently, the study demonstrated that conventions employed in commercial products (e.g., mobile phones and tablets) do not map well to the production domain and should be avoided. These limitations are noted where appropriate.

[0107] The following sections include numerous screenshots, which are provided to communicate the underlying standards and guidelines. Unless specifically noted, these screen designs (e.g., coloring, typography, layout) are offered solely as a means to communicate well established design principles. Interface design is a continuously evolving craft. While specific aesthetic design techniques quickly become passé, most of the underlying principles will persist long after the glimmer of the next fad is gone. Care has been taken to distinguish between design principles and artistic expression. When producing new designs it is important to evaluate the current state of interface design. While the screenshots were arguably "modern" when this standard was authored, we hope the standard's utility will outlive the provided examples; a design's effectiveness is not necessarily a function of its aesthetic quality.

[0108] With this scope established, the following sections will outline the specific components of the standard HMI template as well as general guidance related to graphical interface design in production devices.

6.2 Standard Implementation

[0109] The PackML HMI navigation standard includes: 1) a standard top-level menu including specific icons and labels, 2) a pair of standard layout options for positioning of standard screen elements, and 3) a collection of common, yet optional, supplementary content. These standard HMI components offer the consistency operators need to move efficiently from machine to machine. The following sections illustrate the proper implementation of these components, while concurrently illustrating commonly encountered deviations that should be avoided.

6.2.1 Navigation Contexts

6.3 Guidance

[0135] Allowing operators to efficiently move from machine to machine was the earliest stated goal of this HMI guidance. The standard HMI icons and labels established here, along with the standard set of layout patterns, is an incredible step toward this goal. When presented with a consistent set of icons and labels – each positioned consistently on the screen – operators will more easily identify the content required to complete their HMI interactions. Returning to our earlier definition of consistency, this should be considered external consistency, as it establishes consistency between machines and lines. Internal consistency (i.e., consistency within a single HMI) is equally important. While this standard leaves these aspects to the discretion of the individual designer, the following sections offer additional guidance to help facilitate internal consistency wherever possible. Ironically, we must first discuss contrast to fully appreciate consistency.

6.3.1 Contrast

[0136] We are naturally inclined to identify patterns. We are equally inclined to spot inconsistencies. The lone blue dot in Figure 52 quickly draws our attention. Compared to the remaining dots, it offers a high level of contrast. In this example color is used to highlight the element. This simple change of color produces visual hierarchy within the image. The blue mark is more dominant than the grey marks because of color contrast. When used in an HMI, such applications of color are used to communicate meaning. The blue dot is significantly different; it must be important.

[0137] Figure 53 demonstrates how adding a second highlighted point will split our focus. Two points are now competing for our attention; emphasis on the first point has been reduced. With this additional colored point, a viewer's gaze is more likely to travel around the image. Noting that the marks are the same color, we naturally expect a similar semantic explanation; if we know why the first dot is blue, we assume the second dot is blue for the same reason.

[0138] If a different semantic meaning is required, an alternate color should be introduced; keeping in mind that a good rule-of-thumb suggests no more than 2-3 different colors in an interface. Figure 54 demonstrates the previous example modified to include a second color, which now holds some other meaning. Here, the opposite expectation is in place. If a second color is used, it should have a second meaning. We should not add another color just to make the display "pretty". Given the context of use for our equipment, the operator likely expects alternate colors to have alternate meanings.

[0139] In the preceding examples, color alone was used to produce the intended contrast. When more granularity is required, contrast can also be introduced using size. In Figure 55, with our original dots still highlighted, one now has more focus due to its size; size now means something as well. Our hierarchy is growing...

6.3.2 Squint Testing

[0140] Contrast is used to get the viewers attention. In an HMI, it is important that the amount of contrast is proportional to the element's importance. A quick gauge of the display's hierarchy can be achieved through "squint testing". Simply squint your eyes as you look at an interface. Are the most important points the most salient? Figure 56 simulates a squint test for our running example. The large blue dot is still the clear focal point. As a display expands to include more content, this technique can help the designer maintain a balanced interface. It is important to strike an appropriate balance in contrast. Too little contrast and the viewer has no direction; too much contrast and the viewer's gaze is drawn in too many directions.

6.3.3 Data-Ink Ratio

[0141] Squint testing is merely a technique to validate the previously discussed Data-Ink Ratio concept. The level of contrast introduced by a screen element, should be proportional to the data it communicates. While we have still not established any meaning for our array of dots, it is reasonable to argue that the grey dots are less significant. Their perceived contrast has been limited by reducing their color saturation. Colors in the natural world help establish our perceived correlation between color saturation and proximity. Figure 57 shows a simple landscape of rolling hills. While the most distant hills are lighter with reduced saturation, the nearest hills are darker and more saturated. The distant hills fade until their color almost matches that of the sky. This helps explain why grey is an ideal color for content with minimal importance. Given the white background of this document the grey dots have a low level of contrast. In general, we reduce the perceived contrast of an item by lightening and reducing its level of saturation. In terms of interface hierarchy, reducing contrast pushes an item toward the background, while increasing contrast moves it forward.

[0142] In our array of dots, the highly saturated blue dots have focus, while the grey dots, despite being more numerous, fade to the background. We can employ these same techniques to appropriately balance the components of our interface. Recalling the characteristics of a "usable" interface, we must concurrently offer learnability and efficiency. We have already argued that contrast is necessary to get the operator's attention; in order to afford learnability, we must have contrast. However, once we have learned the interface, any excess contrast becomes a distraction reducing efficiency. If the contrast is overwhelming it does more harm than good. Figure 58 shows the numeric entry box from our Settings-screen example refactored to use a more appropriate level of contrast. Once the screen has been learned, the label "Duration" can fade to the background. We accommodate this by adjusting its size and darkness to reduce the percieved contrast. The same technique is applied to the units label. Units are merely presented to provide context for the associate value, which should be the primary focal point of the control. Accordingly, the emphasis on the units is also reduced; color and size are adjusted appropriately. Finally, the surrounding border, a signifier indicating that the element is clickable, has been likewise muted by reducing the stroke thickness and darkness. The resulting interface is still learnable without the excessive contrast that could serve as a distraction to the viewer. Along the way, it is reasonable to argue that the new box is also more aesthetically pleasing.

[0143] The Data-Ink ratio is used in an iterative fashion to control the importance of screen elements. In our working dot-array example, if necessary, the contrast of the grey marks can be further reduced by inverting their shape as shown in Figure 59. The amount of "ink" has been reduced, leaving a less dominant low contrast mark. When the marks cannot simply by removed all together, reversing polarity is an effective alternative.

[0144] These points all circle back to explain the limitations established for icon usage. Recalling the application notes for the Statistics icon, we intentionally restricted the use of additional borders, faux gridlines, and added marks, which utilized unnecessary "ink" and, therefore, increased contrast without adding any useful semantic meaning. Figure 60 presents a few of our Statistics guidelines. Gratuitous 3D effects and aggressive gradients are the other most commonly abused decorations that push an elements contrast beyond the necessary level of effectiveness.

6.3.4 Signifiers

[0145] With the transition from physical buttons to touch screen displays, designers needed a way to communicate "clickability" to their operators. This was initially achieved by making the on-screen representation of a button look like a physical button. The aesthetic attributes used to communicate "clickability" are termed signifiers; they signify "clickability". Borders that highlight a clickable area are the most commonly encountered signifiers. Today, touchable interfaces are a staple of our daily lives. Accordingly, we are less reliant on the signfiers that were necessary during the transition from physical interfaces. Figure 61 shows a reasonable representation of the evolution of the on-screen button.

[0146] Gone are the button bezels that helped solidify the mental model for operators. Gone are the drop shadows and gradients that mimic light falling on a physical button. Often, now, particularly for icon-based buttons, the surrounding borders that illustrate the clickable area can be removed as well. While there is nothing wrong with the bordered button shown in the middle of Figure 61, the included border is "ink" that can be removed. The bare icon shown on the far right is self-sufficient, communicating "clickable" effectively and efficiently without a surrounding border. With the borders removed, the result is an interface with additional whitespace, which produces a more relaxing tone that operators will likely appreciate. As demonstrated in Figure 62, borders often require ancillary whitespace, which effectively reduces the usable screen area. Removing the borders eliminates the associated congestion and the awkward spacing between elements.

6.3.5 Alignment

[0147] Whereas color offers contrast using light, alignment affords contrast via position. Figure 63 offers two new dot arrays; both grey, but with one key distinction. The grid on the right is perfectly aligned, whereas the grid on the left includes arbitrary alignment deviations. Again, while subtle, these deviations are likely to draw the viewer's attention. This is quite effective if that is the intention, but that is rarely the case. More often, interfaces simply lack the careful alignment that would otherwise bring harmony to the composition.

[0148] In general, we are seeking consistency in alignment; contrast in this dimension should be avoided. This consistency is most commonly achieved using grids, which align and distribute our screen elements effectively. 7. Design Evaluation

7.1 Overcoming Bias

[0146] As a design matures, it is essential to validate its usability. Again, we are discussing more than traditional functional verification. Satisfying, Efficient, Learnable, Memorable, Tolerant: those are our objectives. An interface that makes sense to its designer is not complete until it has been vetted by another set of eyes. The designer's own "it looks good to me" assertion is not a sufficient form of validation. Even the well trained, well intentioned, designer will benefit from some form of supplementary design review. Why?

[0147] We all suffer from bias. One that is particularly challenging – specifically for engineers functioning as designers – is the "curse of knowledge". Coined in Camerer, Loewenstein, and Weber (1989), the curse of knowledge is an inherent inability to see things from the target audience's perspective. The cause of this bias: experience. Despite daily dialogues and interactions with my operators, I simply cannot, with absolute certainty, speak on their behalf. I am not a production line operator. I am an engineer; I just see the world differently. While I empathize and focus on their unique usability requirements, I won't always make the right design decisions. I have seen behind the curtain. I understand the inner workings of my equipment at a level my operator's should never need to comprehend. There is a significant risk that my bias will bleed through; that I will design an interface that is suitable for an engineer, but unsatisfying for an operator. I am more likely to explain away what I feel are simply "quirks" in the interface, which, in the operator's eyes, are more serious usability issues.

[0148] At the very least, I understand that this bias exists. I can use caution as I design; always mindful that my preferences may conflict with the operator's. On the opposite end of the spectrum, without such awareness or caution, bigger problems can occur. The curse of knowledge is responsible for those acronym filled error messages that are easily understood by the design engineer, but completely baffling for the operator using the system. The curse of knowledge helped hide that configuration parameter in the menu you only check after looking in five other places. Worse still, the next time you need that parameter, you may search in all those same wrong places again. I'm not a fool if I mistakenly check the drawer next to my stove every time I am looking for the potato masher. I am only a fool if I fail to move the masher to that drawer once I find it. The operator of a production device has no such luxury. That parameter they are looking for is forever stuck in the drawer the designer thought was "obviously" the right place. Is their interface functional? Yes. Is it satisfying for the operator? No. Is it efficient for the operator? No. To avoid this usability anti-pattern, the designer must do the leg work in advance to determine where the operator thinks that parameter should be.

[0149] To make matters worse, the curse of knowledge grows with the design itself. As the designer molds their interface, they become enamored with its nuance and, unfortunately, comfortable with its flaws. Screens overflowing with content are easily interpreted by the designer that managed to squeeze yet another textbox into the display. Unfortunately, the result is likely overwhelming and completely frustrating to the production operator that is tasked with increasing throughput to hit a quota late in their production shift.

[0150] Fortunately, the cure for this bias is simple; it's humility. Kleon (2012) offered a list of important characteristics that all "artists" should have. As an engineer studying design, I felt that "a willingness to look stupid" was the key. The arrogant engineer, so proud of his work, releases it without the validation it needs. I've been there. "Wow, that looks cool... ship it." I shiver a bit when I think about the designs I've shipped without ever putting them in front of an actual operator. Maybe it's not arrogance. Maybe it is just an unwillingness to look stupid. The mantra of usability design brought forward a new mindset for me. If the operator cannot use my interface, whose fault is it? Mine? Wait... what?

[0151] Balancing display density, information architecture, and aesthetic design is an iterative process. Getting these right the first time is not just challenging, it's unlikely. It doesn't matter how smart you are, or what degrees you have. So, as designers, we often cheat; we need to. We look at existing designs that resonate with us. We model our interfaces after those we find impressive. We figure we will get further faster if we don't reinvent the wheel. Kleon argued just this point, offering the perfect inspiration for those who are aren't "artists", but are tasked with creating "art". The secret, he argued: steal stuff, then make it your own. While I absolutely agree with this thinking – all of my best ideas are someone elses – there are a few risks to keep in mind.

[0152] First, going back to the previous point, I am not an operator. If I find something "intriguing" does that mean my operators will as well? Moreover, will it help them be more effective as an operator? Or will I simply burden them with some clunky widget – like a talking paperclip – that they will just find annoying? Engineers armed with a fancy graphical control library are more likely to gold-plate their interfaces. That 15-slice 3D pie chart, with the fancy animation, is not the best tool to communicate with an an operator. Such tools often impress interface designers without offering their operators any actual benefit. On the contrary, these interfaces make it more difficult to interpret the data, which leads to poor situational awareness and misguided decisions.

[0153] The second risk, which doesn't necessarily apply to the traditional artist, is the nature of our domain. Poor design in our world costs money. An artist grows through creativity; the more carefree the better. An artist cannot be held back by fear. They must take risks; their growth depends on it. They share their work with others openly. They crave the feedback of their peers. That is the humility Kleon described. As engineers, however, our instincts tell us we must get it right the first time. So, if we are going to "steal" ideas for our production equipment, it's probably a good idea to pull insight from companies that have an impressive track record of great design; like Apple. Consider, however, the early versions of Apple's Health App demonstrated in Figure 51. Interesting? Sure. Do I want to force my operators to stare at this all day? Certainly not. We must steal with care. Context is important. That Health App was not designed for use on a piece of industrial equipment. It is not suitable for our domain.

[0154] It is important that we design with the creativity of an artist, while accommodating the risks brought on by the curse of knowledge and the requirements of our domain. We want to produce To do this, we must rely on others to review our work; that is where humility steps in. Our discussion thus far suggests that this is not a simple task. Is it sufficient to simply ask other engineers their opinion? Probably not. Are they operators? Are they graphic designers? Are they impervious to the curse of knowledge? Is the operator at the forefront of their mind? Ideally, we need to put our designs in front of actual operators. Should we do that with our first draft? Then, they too, will have the curse of knowledge. We will have introduced bias; expectation. They cannot see an interface for the first time ever again. Flaws you intend to fix in the future can spoil their first impression. We will have wasted our best chance to actually measure one of our core usability qualities: learnability.
